## Context

AO is the only product in the current portfolio with explicit organic demand signal: inbound interest after the Tauri app post, a job offer triggered by the demo, and a market category that is already pulling in buyers. The answered questions now point to an AO-first plan, with founders spending their human time on customer development and sales rather than trying to launch nine unrelated revenue motions at once.

## Scope

1. Define the AO Pro early access offer in writing: who it is for, what problems it solves, what is included, and what is explicitly deferred
2. Choose an early access pricing model that is simple to explain and easy to sell manually (for example: individual, team, and design-partner tiers)
3. Launch a waitlist/application flow that captures company size, repo volume, current workflow pain, and willingness to pay
4. Set up a founder-led onboarding process for the first cohort: interview, setup session, first workflow run, and weekly follow-up
5. Create a lightweight customer pipeline in the knowledge base or CRM so every lead has a status, owner, next step, and notes
6. Define the first 30-day cohort target and acceptance criteria so the early access program stays small enough to support well

## Success Criteria

- AO Pro early access has a live application flow with clear positioning and pricing
- The first cohort is intentionally capped and has a documented onboarding path
- At least 10 qualified applications or conversations are captured in the first 30 days
- At least 3 design partners complete onboarding and run AO on a real repository
- Founders have a weekly review cadence for pipeline health, conversion blockers, and product feedback

## Notes

- Keep the first release operationally manual; do not block on a full self-serve account system
- The objective is learning and conversion, not polished scale
- Use the strongest proof points repeatedly: local-first, BYOK, multi-model orchestration, worktree isolation, and the 180-PR execution story
