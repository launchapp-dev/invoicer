## Context

Our pricing sits at or below the floor of every competitor category, despite offering more comprehensive products. The SaaS template Lite tier is $149 while ShipFast charges $199 for a simpler product and Supastarter charges up to $1,499. AO Pro is planned at $29/mo while Devin charges $20/mo (but with $400M+ in VC subsidies behind that price). The revenue model emphasizes BYOK (bring your own API keys) which means near-100% margins — yet we're pricing as if we have significant COGS.

Low pricing can signal low value. In developer tools, there's strong evidence that serious buyers (teams, agencies) associate price with quality and support. A $149 template may be perceived as less production-ready than a $349 one.

## What We Know

- Template pricing: $149 Lite, $249 Pro, $599 Team, $1,199 Agency
- Competitor template pricing: ShipFast $199, Makerkit $349/yr, Supastarter $299-$1,499
- AO pricing: Free, $29 Pro, $99 Team, $499 Enterprise
- Competitor agent pricing: Devin $20/mo (VC-subsidized), Factory $20/mo (VC-subsidized)
- Our BYOK model means near-100% gross margins on subscriptions
- Revenue projection: $15K-$60K MRR by month 12
- No pricing experiments or A/B tests mentioned
- No current paying customers to anchor on (per existing question about user evidence)

## What We Don't Know

- What price would maximize revenue (price × volume)?
- Are we competing on price because our product is unproven, or is this a strategic choice?
- Would a higher price ($249-$399 for Lite template) reduce volume but increase revenue?
- Do enterprise buyers have minimum price thresholds below which they won't evaluate a product?
- Is the AO $29/mo price sustainable without VC funding, or should it be $49-$99/mo?
- What's the price elasticity of demand for SaaS templates vs AI agent tools?
- Are we accidentally positioning ourselves as "budget" when we should be "premium"?

## Suggested Investigation

1. **Competitor revenue analysis**: Estimate ShipFast and Makerkit revenue from public signals (testimonials, team size, growth claims) to understand what prices the market bears
2. **Price anchoring experiment**: Launch at $249 for Lite (not $149) and measure conversion — can always discount later, hard to raise prices
3. **Segment pricing**: Check if different buyer segments (indie hackers vs agencies vs enterprises) have different willingness to pay — the $599 Team and $1,199 Agency tiers suggest awareness of this, but the $149 Lite may be cannibalizing
4. **AO pricing benchmark**: Research what enterprises pay for CI/CD tools (GitHub Actions, CircleCI, Jenkins) — AO may be more comparable to DevOps infrastructure ($50-$200/seat/mo) than to code assistants ($20/mo)
5. **Survey potential buyers**: Before launch, ask 20-30 potential customers what they'd expect to pay for these products
