## Context

In three rounds over two days, AI agents generated 79 product ideas (22 new products, 29 features, 28 integrations) with 15 rated 9/10+ priority. The existing question "can a solo founder execute nine revenue streams simultaneously" asks about execution capacity, and "is AI-driven strategy creating an echo chamber" asks about idea quality. But neither asks the meta-question: is the volume of idea generation itself becoming a liability? Strategy debt — like technical debt — accumulates when you generate more options than you can evaluate, prioritize, or discard. Every unexecuted idea is a decision not yet made, and unmade decisions consume cognitive bandwidth.

## What We Know

- 79 ideas were generated across 3 rounds in ~2 days — a rate no human strategist would produce.
- 15 ideas are rated 9/10 or higher priority — the prioritization system is not discriminating enough to create focus.
- The revenue analysis projects $30K–$120K MRR at 12 months across all streams — but this assumes parallel execution of multiple products.
- Each idea includes effort estimates, but no idea includes a "cost of not doing this" or "cost of delay" analysis.
- The "Quick Wins" section lists 9 items — even the "quick" list is too long for a solo founder to execute in parallel.
- Existing strategic questions (35 open) are themselves growing at a rate of 5 per run, adding to the cognitive load.

## What We Don't Know

- What the actual decision process is for converting ideas into committed work — is there a kill/defer mechanism, or does every idea persist indefinitely?
- Whether the 79 ideas have been evaluated against each other (forced ranking, trade-offs) or only scored independently.
- What the opportunity cost of maintaining the ideas backlog is (time spent re-reading, re-evaluating, updating).
- Whether a smaller, more focused set of ideas (e.g., top 5) would produce better outcomes than a comprehensive catalog.
- At what point "idea completeness" tips from strategic asset to decision paralysis.

## Suggested Investigation

- Implement a forced-ranking exercise: of the 79 ideas, which 5 would you bet the company on? Which 50 can be explicitly deferred or killed?
- Add a "status" field to each idea (committed, deferred, killed, speculative) and ruthlessly categorize — most should be "deferred" or "killed."
- Measure time spent by the founder reviewing, updating, and discussing the ideas backlog vs. executing on committed work.
- Consider capping future idea generation rounds: instead of "generate 5 new ideas," use "evaluate and kill 10 existing ideas."
- Establish a maximum "active ideas" limit (e.g., 10) — new ideas can only enter when existing ones are completed or killed.
